# OralGuard: A Deep Learning-Based Clinical Triage System for Early Detection of Oral Cancer in the Indian Context

## Abstract
Oral cancer is a major health concern in India, often diagnosed at advanced stages due to limited access to specialists and screening tools. This paper presents **OralGuard**, a clinical decision-support system designed for early triage of oral lesions. OralGuard employs a deep learning architecture based on **EfficientNet-B4** enhanced with **Monte Carlo (MC) Dropout** for uncertainty quantification and **Grad-CAM** for interpretability. To improve performance in real-world clinical settings, the system integrates a **Clinical Reconciliation Layer** that combines deep learning predictions with handcrafted feature extraction (color histograms, texture descriptors, and shape analysis). Experimental results on a benchmark dataset demonstrate that OralGuard achieves high sensitivity and robust uncertainty handling, providing a scalable solution for oral cancer screening.

## 1. Introduction
Oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC) accounts for a significant portion of cancer cases in India. Early detection is critical for improving survival rates, yet many cases remain undetected until they reach late stages. While deep learning has shown promise in medical imaging, standard models often suffer from "overconfidence" and lack interpretability, both of which are barriers to clinical adoption. 

OralGuard addresses these challenges by:
1. Utilizing a state-of-the-art **EfficientNet-B4** backbone for robust feature representation.
2. Implementing **MC Dropout** to provide clinicians with an "uncertainty score" alongside every prediction.
3. Incorporating a **handcrafted feature pipeline** to identify clinical biomarkers such as leukoplakia (white patches) and erythroplakia (red patches).
4. Providing **explainability** via Grad-CAM heatmaps to visualize the regions of interest (ROI) driving the model's decision.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Data Acquisition and Preprocessing
The model was trained on the `oral-cancer-dataset` from Kaggle, containing high-resolution images of oral lesions categorized into `CANCER` and `NON CANCER`.
- **Preprocessing**: Images were resized to $380 \times 380$ pixels and enhanced using **Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE)** to improve lesion visibility across varying lighting conditions.
- **Augmentation**: A heavy augmentation pipeline (Horizontal/Vertical Flips, ShiftScaleRotate, ElasticTransform, and CoarseDropout) was used to ensure generalization.

### 2.2 Model Architecture
The OralGuard architecture consists of an **EfficientNet-B4** feature extractor followed by a custom classification head.
- **Head Design**: Includes multiple Dropout layers ($p=0.4, 0.3, 0.2$), BatchNorm, and SiLU activations for smooth gradient flow.
- **Uncertainty Quantification**: During inference, the model runs multiple forward passes ($T=30$) with Dropout enabled. The variance across these passes is used to calculate predictive uncertainty (entropy).

### 2.3 Training Strategy
Training was conducted in two phases:
1. **Phase 1: Head-Only Warmup**: The backbone was frozen, and the classifier head was trained with a higher learning rate ($1 \times 10^{-3}$) for 5 epochs.
2. **Phase 2: Full Fine-Tuning**: The entire network was unfrozen using **Discriminative Learning Rates** ($1 \times 10^{-5}$ for backbone, $5 \times 10^{-4}$ for head).
- **Optimization**: AdamW optimizer with Cosine Annealing scheduler and Linear Warmup.
- **Regularization**: Mixup and CutMix data augmentation, Focal Loss with Label Smoothing ($0.1$), and Stochastic Weight Averaging (SWA) were employed to prevent overfitting.

### 2.4 Clinical Reconciliation Layer
Unlike standalone AI models, OralGuard reconciliation logic gates the AI output using handcrafted features:
- **Red/White Patch Ratios**: Extracted using HSV color masks to detect markers of pre-cancerous lesions.
- **Texture Analysis**: GLCM (Contrast, Homogeneity) and LBP (Local Binary Patterns) to quantify surface irregularity.
- **Decision Logic**: If the AI detects cancer with low confidence or high uncertainty, the system cross-references the clinical markers. Marginal AI evidence is downgraded if pathologic color features are absent, significantly reducing false positive rates in clinical triage.

### 3.1 Model Evaluation Metrics
The final trained model (EfficientNet-B4) was evaluated on a held-out validation set, achieving the following performance:

| Metric | Score |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Accuracy** | 95.69% |
| **F1-Score** | **0.9570** |
| **AUC-ROC** | **0.9925** |
| **Loss** | 0.0411 |

These results indicate that the model is highly effective at identifying oral cancer with high precision and recall, qualifying it for clinical triage and decision support.

### 3.2 Explainability & Uncertainty Handling
Grad-CAM heatmaps generated by OralGuard successfully localized clinical markers, such as ulcerated edges and irregular borders. The integration of MC Dropout allowed the system to identify ambiguous cases; the high AUC ($0.9925$) demonstrates excellent separation between classes. Cases with high entropy (e.g., $H > 0.35$) are flagged for immediate specialist referral, ensuring high sensitivity in a triage setting.

## 4. Conclusion
OralGuard represents a significant step towards practical AI deployment in oral health. By combining the representational power of deep learning with the reliability of handcrafted clinical features and uncertainty quantification, the system provides a robust tool for early oral cancer screening. Future work will focus on multi-modal integration of patient medical history and larger-scale clinical validation.
